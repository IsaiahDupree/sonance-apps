System requirements

  • macOS 14 Sonoma or later. vrdesk uses ScreenCaptureKit and modern capture APIs.
  • Apple Silicon or Intel Mac. Apple Silicon encodes the video stream most efficiently, but Intel works too.
  • Meta Quest 3 or Quest 3S on the same local network as your Mac.
  • For the smoothest video, put your Mac on Ethernet. Wi-Fi-to-Wi-Fi can choke the stream; a wired Mac with the headset on Wi-Fi is the reliable setup.

Install & set up

  1. Open the downloaded vrdesk-0.9.0.dmg.
  2. Drag vrdesk into your Applications folder.
  3. Launch vrdesk from Applications (first launch: right-click the app and choose Open).
  4. When onboarding asks, grant Screen Recording so vrdesk can capture your desktop to stream.
  5. Grant Accessibility so your headset can drive your Mac's keyboard and mouse.
  6. Read the 6-digit pairing code from the vrdesk icon in your menu bar — you'll type it into the headset.

The two permissions macOS will ask for

Screen Recording

macOS requires this before any app can read screen pixels. vrdesk uses it to capture your desktop and encode it into the video stream your headset displays. Without it, your headset shows a black screen.

Accessibility

This lets vrdesk inject keyboard and mouse input, so a controller, tracked hand, or Bluetooth keyboard in the headset can actually click and type on your Mac. Without it, the view works but stays read-only.

Both are granted by you in System Settings → Privacy & Security, and you can revoke either one at any time. The captured screen and injected input only ever travel to the headset you paired with, over your local network — never to us, and never to any server. On macOS 15 Sequoia, Apple re-asks for Screen Recording approval periodically; if your stream goes black after an update, re-approve vrdesk in System Settings and relaunch.

Pair with your headset

Put on your Quest, open the vrdesk headset app, and it will discover your Mac on the network automatically. When it asks, enter the 6-digit code shown in your Mac's menu bar. That code is what gates the connection — your Mac only starts streaming to a headset that entered the right code.

Troubleshooting

macOS won't let me open the app

The app is signed and notarized, but on first launch Gatekeeper can still be cautious. Right-click (or Control-click) vrdesk in Applications and choose Open, then confirm once. You won't be asked again.

The video stalls or freezes after the first frame

This is almost always Wi-Fi-to-Wi-Fi congestion. Put your Mac on Ethernet and keep the headset on Wi-Fi — the wired link clears it up. Staying on the 5 GHz band and close to the router also helps.

I can see my desktop but the keyboard and mouse do nothing

Grant Accessibility to vrdesk in System Settings → Privacy & Security → Accessibility, then relaunch. That permission is what lets the headset control your Mac.

How do I get updates?

For this version, updates are a manual re-download — grab the latest .dmg from this page and drag the new app over the old one. Your settings are preserved.

Where does my screen go — is it private?

Your screen never leaves your local network. vrdesk connects your Mac and headset peer-to-peer with WebRTC; there is no cloud server, no relay, and no analytics. See the privacy policy for the full detail.
